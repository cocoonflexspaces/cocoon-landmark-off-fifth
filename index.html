The building at a glance 03

A purpose-built exhibition house, a century in the making.

Designed by architect Ogden Codman and commissioned by Archer Milton Huntington in 1913–1915 as a library, exhibition hall, entertainment space and studio for the sculptor Anna Hyatt Huntington. Gifted to the National Academy in 1941, it exhibited fine art for over half a century — then was fully restored and reopened in 2021 as a contemporary exhibition space.

Why producers ask about it

The address. Directly across from the Guggenheim, steps off Fifth Avenue and Central Park — guests arrive at one of the most recognizable museum blocks in the world.

A covered porte cochère. A drive-in arrival and load-in court behind landmark wood doors — a near-impossible luxury in Manhattan, equally good for talent arrivals and production load-in.

Museum-grade galleries. Three floors of restored exhibition rooms served by a passenger / service elevator, each with its own character: white-box, carved stone, and warm wood panelling.

It reads on camera. The oval stair, the checkered marble, the arched windows — the building photographs like a set that took a hundred years to dress.

Second floor · Stone Room & Gallery 2 05

The showpiece floor, under 17-foot ceilings.

The Stone Room is the building's signature: carved Caen stone walls, original checkered marble floors and arched windows over the street. It opens onto Gallery 2 — the same soaring ceiling height in a clean, restored-wood-floor gallery.
